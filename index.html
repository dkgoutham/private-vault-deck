Two privacy layers, one architecture

The system has two separate privacy domains, plus the unmodified public vault.

$\text{SPP}[A]$, the asset pool

One pool per underlying asset $A$. Holds asset custody. Provides the big anonymity set for private deposits in $A$.

$\text{PV}[V]$, the share pool

One per public vault $V$. Holds $V$'s ERC-4626 shares. Verifies all SNARKs. Orchestrates every cross-contract call.

$V$, the public vault

Plain ERC-4626, untouched. Sees only aggregate calls from $\text{SPP}[A]$ and $\text{PV}[V]$. No notion of users.

Anonymity-set-per-asset (not per-vault) is why $\text{SPP}[A]$ is separated from $\text{PV}[V]$. If each vault had its own asset pool, the anonymity set would fragment across vaults; with a universal $\text{SPP}[A]$, all WETH depositors share one set regardless of which vault they ultimately enter.

Off-chain (dotted)

  • SPP[A] → V: V.deposit   PV[V] → V: V.redeem
The encrypted intent plaintexts go on-chain as calldata of spendAsset / spendShare. Aggregators don't receive them out-of-band, they read events from SPP[A] (entry intents) and PV[V] (exit intents) and try to decrypt with their viewing key. That's why the dotted lines go from Aggregator to the contracts, not from User to Aggregator.

Threat model. Privacy is cryptographic against the public observer and the relayer. The aggregator is the residual trust assumption: it learns target vault and amount for the requests it batches, but it cannot fulfil them on a user's behalf. The user's spending authority stays with the user.

Drive home: aggregator can read and batch but cannot move funds. The crypto researcher should leave this slide knowing exactly which party is the residual trust boundary and why it's bounded (read-only, not spend).

Building blocks: notes, commitments, trees, nullifiers

The shared mechanics every pool in the system uses, before we get into anything entry-specific.

Notes and commitments

AssetNote: private UTXO holding value $v$ of an underlying asset, payable to address $pk_d$.

ShareNote: private UTXO holding share count $s$ of vault $V$.

Note commitment $cm$: a Pedersen hash of the note's opening. Hides $v$ (or $s$) and $pk_d$. The opening stays in the user's wallet; only $cm$ goes on chain.

Trees and anchors

Commitment tree $T_{cm}$: an incremental Merkle tree of every commitment ever inserted in a pool. $\text{SPP}[A]$ has $T_{cm}[A]$, $\text{PV}[V]$ has $T_{cm}[V]$.

Anchor: a recent root of $T_{cm}$. Every spend proof picks an anchor and proves Merkle membership against it.

Spend lifecycle and nullifiers

Spend = consume old notes, produce new ones. The user proves ownership, Merkle membership, and value conservation.

Nullifier $nf$: a hash derived from the note's secrets and the user's spending key. Published on spend; inserted into the pool's nullifier set $\mathcal{N}$.

Double-spend prevention: each pool refuses to insert any $nf$ already in $\mathcal{N}$. Once a nullifier is in, the corresponding note is unspendable forever.

$\mathcal{N}[A]$ lives in $\text{SPP}[A]$; $\mathcal{N}[V]$ lives in $\text{PV}[V]$.

This is the standard Zcash-style note + commitment-tree + nullifier scheme. Crypto reviewers know this pattern. Spend 30 seconds confirming familiarity, then move on. The next slide specialises this for intents and receipts.

Building blocks: intent notes and receipts

Two specialisations on top of the shared mechanics. Both are central to entry and exit.

The receipt

Opening (Alice's wallet, never on chain):

$R_{\text{ent}} = (\text{assetId},\, \text{vaultId},\, x,\, claimPk,\, \text{expiry},\, \rho_R)$

All parameters of Alice's request, plus secrets only she holds ($claimPk$ and the receipt trapdoor $\rho_R$).

Commitment (the on-chain handle):

$rc_{\text{ent}} = \text{ReceiptCommit}_{\text{ent}}(R_{\text{ent}})$

Hiding Poseidon commitment to $R_{\text{ent}}$. Reveals nothing about $x$ or $claimPk$. This is Alice's claim ticket.

The entry intent note

A note (just like an AssetNote) with a restricted authorisation type:

\[\begin{aligned}\text{EntryIntentNote} = (\,&\text{authType=EntrySink},\, \text{assetId},\, \text{vaultId},\, x, \\ &rc_{\text{ent}},\, \text{refundPk},\, \text{expiry},\, \rho,\, rcm\,)\end{aligned}\]

Carries $rc_{\text{ent}}$ inside its preimage, binding the on-chain intent to Alice's off-chain receipt.

Its commitment $cm_{\text{intent}}$ goes into $T_{cm}[A]$. Bit-indistinguishable from any other commitment.

Cannot be spent by key. Only the execution circuit (during a batch) or the refund circuit (after expiry) can consume it.

Why two objects, not one?

The intent note is the aggregator's input. It signals "this user wants to put $x$ WETH into vault $V$". The aggregator picks it up alongside other intents, sums them, and submits one combined deposit into the public vault. The intent itself is consumed when the batch executes.

The receipt is the user's claim ticket. Alice keeps it, then later uses it to claim her share of the vault position from $\text{PV}[V]$, in proportion to how much she contributed.

Two objects, two roles: the intent feeds the aggregator's batched call; the receipt lets the user pull her share back out afterwards.

The bifurcation: intent note goes into the aggregate vault deposit (consumed there); receipt is the user's own private handle to claim shares back from the private vault later. Don't mention RCroot here, the audience hasn't seen it yet.

Entry protocol

Three phases: intent → execution → claim

running example

Alice holds an AssetNote of $X = 5$ WETH.
She wants to commit $x = 2$ WETH into vault $V$.
The batch has $m = 4$ intents totalling $A_{\text{in}} = 10$ WETH.
$V$ mints $S_{\text{out}} = 10$ shares to $\text{PV}[V]$.
Alice's pro-rata allocation: $s = \lfloor 2 \cdot 10 / 10 \rfloor = \mathbf{2}$ shares.
Anchor the running example here. Every constraint slide will refer back to it.

Intent: Alice's local construction

1. Sample fresh secrets

claimSk  ←$  𝔽    claimPk  = [claimSk]·B
ρ_R      ←$  𝔽    (receipt trapdoor)
ρ        ←$  𝔽    rcm     ←$  𝔽    (intent note)
refundSk ←$  𝔽    refundPk = [refundSk]·B

2. Fill in the structures from the previous slide

Compute $R_{\text{ent}}$ and $rc_{\text{ent}} = \text{ReceiptCommit}_{\text{ent}}(R_{\text{ent}})$. Build the EntryIntentNote with $rc_{\text{ent}}$, $\text{refundPk}$, $\text{expiry}$ embedded.

3. Build a change note

If $x < X$, build a fresh $\text{AssetNote}(X-x, \ldots)$ at a new $pk_d$. Both new commitments will be inserted into $T_{cm}[A]$ in one transaction.

Alice's example:
$X = 5$, $x = 2$, change $= 3$
Each random ($\rho_R, \rho, claimSk, \ldots$) is sampled fresh per intent.
She'll need a change AssetNote of value 3 at a fresh $pk_d$.

Three private secrets, three roles

  • $\rho$: keeps the execution nullifier $nf_{\text{ent}}$ unforgeable
  • $\rho_R$: hides the receipt opening in $rc_{\text{ent}}$
  • $claimSk$: gates who can later claim the pro-rata share
Stress: $rc_{\text{ent}}$ never appears in plaintext on chain at request time. It's published later, as part of the executed batch's RCroot. The aggregator sees $x$ and vaultId because they decrypt the intent, but they never see $R_{\text{ent}}$ or $claimSk$.

The proof Alice generates

$\pi_{\text{spend}}$ is a SNARK proving:

1.The input AssetNote really exists in $T_{cm}[A]$ at a recent anchor.Merkle membership of $cm_{\text{old}}$.
2.Alice owns it.Knowledge of $sk_u$ matching the note's $pk_d$.
3.The published spend nullifier $nf_{\text{old,asset}}$ is the canonical PRF output.$nf_{\text{old,asset}} = \text{PRF}^{\text{nf}}_{nk_{\text{nf}}}(\text{assetId}, \rho_{\text{old}})$
4.The new intent commitment and change commitment are well-formed.Both Pedersen commitments computed from the witness opening.
5.Value conservation: $x + (X - x) = X$.No wei minted out of thin air, no wei lost.

Public inputs: $(nf_{\text{old,asset}},\, cm_{\text{intent,ent}},\, cm_{\text{change}},\, \text{anchor})$. Witnesses: the AssetNote opening, $sk_u$, $\rho_{\text{old}}$, the new intent and change openings.

On-chain: SPP[A].spendAsset

spendAsset(nf_old, cm_intent, cm_change?, π_spend, {(epk_j, C_j)}_{j=1..k}):
  1. require verify(π_spend, public)
  2. require nf_old ∉ N[A];   N[A].insert(nf_old)
  3. T_cm[A].insert(cm_intent)
  4. if cm_change present: T_cm[A].insert(cm_change)
  5. emit IntentCreated(cm_intent, {(epk_j, C_j)})

What just happened on-chain

  • Alice's old AssetNote is irreversibly spent ($nf_{\text{old}} \in \mathcal{N}[A]$).
  • An EntryIntentNote sits in $T_{cm}[A]$, indistinguishable bit-wise from any other commitment.
  • A change AssetNote sits beside it, also in $T_{cm}[A]$.

What aggregators do off-chain

  • Each aggregator $j$ watches `IntentCreated`, attempts to decrypt $C_j$.
  • On success: index the opening by $\text{vaultId}$.
  • Wait until $m$ intents accumulate for some vault, then go to execution.

Sidebar: why intent notes can't be spent by key

Every commitment carries a hidden $\text{authType} \in \{\text{UserOwned},\, \text{EntrySink},\, \text{ExitSink}\}$ inside its preimage. Public commitments are bit-indistinguishable across types.

MechanismEffect
Each pool registers a fixed verifier per entry point (`spendAsset` vs `executeEntry` vs `refundEntry`).Prover cannot pick the verifier.
Spend circuit constrains output $\text{authType}$ to match its destination role.UserOwned for asset/share outputs; EntrySink/ExitSink for intent outputs.
Execution circuit constrains input $\text{authType} = \text{EntrySink}$ / $\text{ExitSink}$.UserOwned notes can't be fed to executor.

Together this rules out: (i) spending an intent note by key (no verifier accepts it as UserOwned input); (ii) feeding a UserOwned note to executor; (iii) forging an "intent-shaped" pre-image with $\text{authType}=\text{UserOwned}$ to bypass sink rules.

This is a small slide but a question magnet. authType is what makes "consumable only by execution or refund circuit" enforceable cryptographically rather than by ABI convention.

Execution: assemble the batch

Off-chain (relayer + aggregator)

  1. Receive openings of $m$ intents for the same $\text{vaultId}$.
  2. Compute $A_{\text{in}} = \sum_i x_i$.
  3. Compute $\text{RCroot} = \text{MerkleRoot}([rc_{\text{ent},i}])$.
  4. Pick $\text{anchor}$ ∈ $T_{cm}[A].\text{validRoots}$ (recent root).
  5. Generate $\pi_{\text{exec,ent}}$.
Running batch:
Alice: $x_1 = 2$   Bob: $x_2 = 3$
Carol: $x_3 = 1$   Dan: $x_4 = 4$
$m = 4,\quad A_{\text{in}} = 10$
$\text{RCroot} = \text{MerkleRoot}(rc_1, rc_2, rc_3, rc_4)$

Public inputs vs witnesses

Public: $\big(\text{anchor},\, \text{assetId},\, \text{vaultId},\, A_{\text{in}},\, \{nf_{\text{ent},i}\}_{i=1..m},\, \text{RCroot},\, m\big)$

Witness: $\{\text{EntryIntentNote}_i,\, x_i,\, rc_{\text{ent},i},\, \rho_i,\, rcm_i,\, \text{MerklePath}_i\}_{i=1..m}$

Subscript $i$ means "field $i$ supplied as witness." A bare $\text{vaultId}$ is the public input claimed for the whole batch.

Circuit constraints (a) to (d)

#Constraint (notation)What it ensures
(a) $\forall i:\ cm_{\text{intent},i} = \text{NoteCommit}(\text{"entry-intent"} \| \text{assetId} \| \text{vaultId} \| x_i \| rc_{\text{ent},i} \| \ldots \| \rho_i \| rcm_i)$ Re-committing the witness opening reproduces the on-chain commitment. Binds $x_i$, $rc_{\text{ent},i}$, $\rho_i$ to the leaf.
(b) $\forall i:\ cm_{\text{intent},i} \in \text{Tree}(\text{anchor})$ via $\text{MerklePath}_i$ Each consumed leaf actually exists in $T_{cm}[A]$. Prevents fabricating intents.
(c) $\forall i:\ \text{authType}_i = \text{EntrySink}\ \land\ \text{assetId}_i = \text{assetId}\ \land\ \text{vaultId}_i = \text{vaultId}$ Each leaf is an entry intent (not an asset spend), and is targeted at this asset and this vault. Prevents cross-asset / cross-vault execution.
(d) $\forall i:\ nf_{\text{ent},i} = H^{\text{nf,ent}}(\text{vaultId},\, \text{assetId},\, \rho_i)$ Published nullifier matches the canonical hash. Otherwise relayer could publish arbitrary nullifiers,colliding with future legit intents or skipping consumed ones.

Why an unkeyed hash for $nf_{\text{ent}}$? Intent notes have no spending key,the aggregator must produce $nf_{\text{ent}}$ on the user's behalf. Unforgeability comes from $\rho_i$'s confidentiality, not from a key.

Circuit constraints (e) to (g) : soundness core

#ConstraintWhat it ensures
(e) $\forall i:\ \text{currentEpoch} \le \text{expiry}_i$ Each intent is still within validity window. After expiry, the refund path is the only legal consumer of the same nullifier.
(f) $A_{\text{in}} = \sum_{i=1}^{m} x_i$ The publicly claimed aggregate equals the actual sum. Relayer cannot inflate or misreport the deposit total.
(g) $\text{RCroot} = \text{MerkleRoot}(rc_{\text{ent},1}, \ldots, rc_{\text{ent},m})$ Critical clause. Receipt root is bound exactly to the receipts inside the consumed intents: no extras, no omissions, no duplicates.

Why (g) is the soundness clause for receipts

Suppose a malicious relayer wants to omit Alice's receipt, duplicate Bob's, or substitute one receipt for another in $\text{RCroot}$.

  • Substituting a different $rc$ breaks (a): the substituted $rc$ wouldn't reproduce the intent's $cm$.
  • Including an $rc$ not from a consumed intent breaks (b): that intent isn't in the tree.
  • Omitting Alice's $rc$ breaks (g): the root won't equal what the circuit computes.

Without (g), the entire claim system is unsound,Alice could be excluded from her own batch's RCroot.

On-chain execution

// Under PV[V]'s reentrancy lock,V hooks cannot re-enter
PV[V].executeEntry(epochId, π_exec_ent, publicInputs):
  1. require publicInputs.currentEpoch == on-chain time source
  2. require verify(π_exec_ent, publicInputs)
  3. pre  = IERC20(V).balanceOf(address(this))      // share balance
  4. SPP[A].depositToVault(vaultId, A_in, {nf_ent_i}, anchor)
       └─ require msg.sender == registeredPVs[assetId]
       └─ require assetId == this.assetId
       └─ require anchor ∈ T_cm[A].validRoots
       └─ for each nf: require ∉ N[A]; insert
       └─ WETH.approve(V, A_in)
       └─ V.deposit(A_in, receiver = PV[V])
       └─ WETH.approve(V, 0)                        // residual reset
  5. post = IERC20(V).balanceOf(address(this))
  6. S_out = post − pre;  require S_out > 0
  7. EntryEpoch[epochId] = (vaultId, assetId, A_in, S_out, RCroot, m)
  8. emit EntryEpochExecuted(...) and EntryReceiptList(epochId, [rc_1..rc_m])
Running example:
$A_{\text{in}} = 10$ WETH → $V$
$V$ mints $S_{\text{out}} = 10$ shares → $\text{PV}[V]$
Stored: $\text{EntryEpoch}[E] = (V, \text{WETH}, 10, 10, \text{RCroot}, 4)$

Why pre/post balance, not return value?

$V$ is untrusted for privacy and economically risky. The authoritative $S_{\text{out}}$ is what $\text{PV}[V]$ actually owns after the call, not what $V$ claims to have minted.

The two events users index on

Step 8 emits two events. Together they let any user claim without trusting an off-chain party.

EntryEpochExecuted

$(\text{epochId},\, \text{vaultId},\, A_{\text{in}},\, S_{\text{out}},\, \text{RCroot},\, m)$

$A_{\text{in}}, S_{\text{out}}$divisor and dividend of every user's pro-rata allocation
$\text{RCroot}$the receipt-list root every claim proof binds against
$\text{epochId}, \text{vaultId}, m$identifiers and batch size

EntryReceiptList

$(\text{epochId},\, [rc_{\text{ent},1}, \ldots, rc_{\text{ent},m}])$

  • Full ordered receipt list, emitted in calldata.
  • Each $rc_i$ is a hiding Poseidon commitment. Publishing the list reveals nothing about per-user amounts or claim secrets.
  • Anyone can rebuild the Merkle tree and produce $\text{MerklePath}_{rc}$ for any leaf.

Why both events must be on-chain

Without EntryEpochExecuted, the public values $A_{\text{in}}, S_{\text{out}}, \text{RCroot}$ aren't available to bind a claim proof to.

Without EntryReceiptList, Alice would need a trusted index from the relayer to find her position. A malicious relayer could withhold her index and her receipt would sit unclaimable in $\text{RCroot}$.

With both on-chain, the data is permanent and every user has a self-contained path from receipt to claim.

Tie back to constraint (g): (g) binds RCroot to exactly these receipts; this slide adds that the list itself is also on-chain, so the binding is publicly checkable. The two events together give every user a self-contained claim.

Claim: Alice mints her ShareNote

Discovery (off-chain)

  1. Watch `EntryEpochExecuted(epochId, vaultId, A_in, S_out, RCroot, m)`.
  2. Read corresponding `EntryReceiptList(epochId, rc_list)`.
  3. Compute $rc_{\text{ent}}$ from her stored $R_{\text{ent}}$.
  4. Find index $i$ with $\text{rc\_list}[i] = rc_{\text{ent}}$.
  5. Build $\text{MerklePath}_{rc}$ from leaf $i$ to $\text{RCroot}$.

Fresh material for output

  • $\rho_s, rcm_s \xleftarrow{\$} \mathbb{F}$
  • fresh diversifier $d \xleftarrow{\$} \{0,1\}^{88}$
  • derive a fresh diversified address $pk_d^{\text{user}}$ from her keys and $d$
Alice's pro-rata: \[ s = \left\lfloor \frac{x \cdot S_{\text{out}}}{A_{\text{in}}} \right\rfloor = \left\lfloor \frac{2 \cdot 10}{10} \right\rfloor = 2 \]

Claim nullifier (single-use):

$cnf_{\text{ent}} = \text{PRF}^{\text{claim}}_{claimSk}(\text{"entry"}, E, rc_{\text{ent}})$

$claimSk$ is held only by Alice. The $cnf$ binds her single claim to (epoch, receipt). Prevents double-claim of the same receipt.

New ShareNote:

$cm_{\text{share,out}} = \text{NoteCommit}(\text{"share"} \| \text{vaultId} \| s \| pk_d^{\text{user}} \| \rho_s \| rcm_s)$

Claim circuit constraints C1 to C4

#ConstraintWhat it ensures
C1 $rc_{\text{ent}} = \text{ReceiptCommit}_{\text{ent}}(R_{\text{ent}})$ The witness opening Alice supplies really opens to the receipt commitment the proof binds to.
C2 $rc_{\text{ent}} \in \text{RCroot}$ via $\text{MerklePath}_{rc}$ $rc_{\text{ent}}$ was one of the $m$ receipts in this executed epoch,the receipt list authority chain.
C3 $R_{\text{ent}}.\text{vaultId} = \text{vaultId}$ The receipt's vault matches this epoch's vault. Prevents claiming Vault-X's epoch with a Vault-Y receipt.
C4 $R_{\text{ent}}.\text{expiry} \ge \text{epochId}$ Receipt was still valid at the time of execution (sanity check; mirrors execution constraint (e) on the claim side).

C1+C2 together is the chain of authority: opening matches commitment, commitment is in the executed epoch's RCroot. Everything else hangs off this.

Claim circuit constraints C5 to C8

#ConstraintWhat it ensures
C5 $claimPk = [claimSk]\cdot B$ Alice knows the claim secret committed in the receipt. Anyone who saw $rc_{\text{ent}}$ but not $claimSk$ cannot forge the claim.
C6 $cnf_{\text{ent}} = \text{PRF}^{\text{claim}}_{claimSk}(\text{"entry"} \| \text{epochId} \| rc_{\text{ent}})$ The published claim nullifier is the canonical PRF output. Combined with $\mathcal{N}^{\text{claim,ent}}[V]$ membership check on-chain, enforces single-use.
C7 $s = \lfloor x \cdot S_{\text{out}} / A_{\text{in}} \rfloor$ Allocation is exactly the pro-rata floor. Alice has no freedom to inflate; $x$ is bound by $rc_{\text{ent}}$ via $R_{\text{ent}}$, $A_{\text{in}}/S_{\text{out}}$ are public epoch values.
C8 $cm_{\text{share,out}} = \text{NoteCommit}(\text{"share"} \| \text{vaultId} \| s \| pk_d^{\text{user}} \| \rho_s \| rcm_s)$ The new share-note commitment is well-formed and binds $s$ to Alice's chosen recipient address.

Alice's only degree of freedom at claim time

The diversified address $pk_d^{\text{user}}$. Everything else (allocation $s$, claim nullifier, output commitment) is determined by $R_{\text{ent}}$ and the epoch's public record. The output randomness $(\rho_s, rcm_s)$ is fresh but value-irrelevant,it just randomizes the new commitment.

This freedom is intentional: it gives the new ShareNote unlinkability from Alice's prior on-chain addresses.

On-chain claim & epoch close

PV[V].claimEntry(epochId, cnf_ent, cm_share_out, π_claim_ent):
  1. require cnf_ent ∉ N_claim_ent[V]
  2. require EntryEpoch[epochId].RCroot ≠ 0
  3. fetch (vaultId, A_in, S_out, RCroot) from EntryEpoch[epochId]
  4. require verify(π_claim_ent, public)
  5. T_cm[V].insert(cm_share_out)
  6. N_claim_ent[V].insert(cnf_ent)
  7. emit ShareNoteCreated(cm_share_out)
Alice's outcome:
$cm_{\text{share,out}}$ inserted into $T_{cm}[V]$ at a fresh $pk_d^{\text{user}}$.
$cnf_{\text{ent}}$ added to $\mathcal{N}^{\text{claim,ent}}[V]$.
Alice now holds a $\text{ShareNote}(s = 2)$, spendable like any UserOwned note.

Aggregate share conservation

$\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^{m} s_i \le S_{\text{out}}$

Each $s_i$ bound by C7 to the floor; summing $m$ floors can never exceed $S_{\text{out}}$. Contract doesn't need a running sum. Residual is dust held by $\text{PV}[V]$.

Refund & liveness

Every intent carries $(\text{refundPk},\, \text{expiry})$. After expiry, the holder of $\text{refundSk}$ can recover.

Mutual exclusion via shared $nf$

Execution and refund consume the same $nf_{\text{ent}}$ (or $nf_{\text{xit}}$).

  • Whichever transaction is mined first writes the nullifier; the other reverts.
  • After expiry, execution circuit's constraint (e) rejects → only refund is live.
  • Aggregator can delay, but cannot strand past expiry.

If a race causes a revert

If Alice's refund reverts because execution landed first, her receipt is in some epoch's RCroot, so she switches to the claim path. Either way, no funds stuck.

Wallet responsibility

Backup of $(R_{\text{ent}}, claimSk, refundSk, \rho, rcm)$ is wallet-local. Self-encrypted on-chain backup is an engineering option.

Takeaways

  1. Architecture: universal $\text{SPP}[A]$ for asset anonymity + per-vault $\text{PV}[V]$ for share custody. $V$ is unmodified.
  2. The receipt: $rc_{\text{ent}}$ is a hidden Poseidon commitment Alice keeps. It binds $x$ and $claimSk$ before the batch is even formed.
  3. The soundness core: constraint (g) binds $\text{RCroot}$ to exactly the receipts inside the consumed intents. The entire claim system rests on this clause.
  4. The pro-rata bind: C1+C2 (opening matches commitment, commitment is in the epoch's RCroot) + C7 (allocation is the floored pro-rata) leaves Alice exactly one degree of freedom: her diversified recipient address.
  5. Residual trust: honest-but-curious aggregator. It sees the plaintext of intents it batched. Threshold committees over the viewing keys can close this gap.
